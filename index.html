Useful, Not Just Plausible

Bridging Physics Simulation and Learned World Models for Robot Control

CoRL 2026 | Austin, Texas, USA | November 9, 2026
Email: corl26physwm@gmail.com
JW Marriott Austin (110 E 2nd St, Austin, TX 78701)

Overview

Recent advancements in learned world models have demonstrated remarkable success in generating visually plausible futures from static images and videos. However, for interaction-rich robot intelligence, especially robot manipulation, visual plausibility is vastly insufficient. A model that perfectly predicts pixel flow may fundamentally fail to capture the discontinuous contact dynamics, frictional constraints, and underactuated physics required for reliable action selection and closed-loop control. On the other hand, analytical physics simulations have been enabling robotic control and planning to capture the underlying physics, though they lack the generality that learned models offer. This workshop challenges the prevailing trend of purely generative world modeling by asking a critical question: How can analytical physics simulation enhance learned world models for better robotic control?

We aim to bridge the historically disparate communities of physics simulation and deep representation learning and world modeling. The workshop will critically examine whether scaling up world modeling based on videos will be enough to capture the complex physics involved in robot control and ensure robust generalization in real-world scenarios. By bringing together experts in simulator development, robot control, and foundation models, we will also together explore how to inject structural physics priors into learned representations, how to integrate physics simulators with learned world models, how robots can exploit imperfect models for control, and what evaluation benchmarks can finally separate visually convincing models from dynamically actionable ones.

This half-day event is highly relevant to the CoRL community as it targets the immediate bottleneck in deploying foundation models to physical hardware. The intended audience includes researchers and practitioners actively struggling with the sim-to-real gap, long-horizon compounding errors, and the integration of neural world models into robot learning and control pipelines.

Core Challenges / Research Questions

This workshop will focus on the following specific research questions at the intersection of world models, physics simulation, and robot learning:

  • Q1 — The Representation Bottleneck: Can physical understanding emerge from training purely on videos? Recent generative models predict visually plausible futures, but visual prediction is vastly insufficient for interaction. What physical quantities (e.g., discontinuous contact, frictional constraints, contact forces) cannot be reliably inferred from passive video data? What benchmarks and evaluation protocols are needed to prove a model captures the underactuated physics required for real manipulation and locomotion, rather than just generating convincing pixels? How do we scale up other modalities for generative world models, such as tactile data?
  • Q2 — Should physical priors be injected into learned world models? If so, how? The bitter lesson suggests that designing and injecting manually created priors will eventually lose compared to scaling up. Is this true in the context of world models built for robotic control? If physical priors are still necessary, at least in the short run, how can we introduce such priors without restricting learning?
  • Q3 — Bridging the Gap: How can analytical simulation and neural world models be integrated? Should physics simulators be relegated to providing pre-training data, or should they provide structural priors, differentiable objectives, and regularizers for learned world models? Conversely, how can learned representations be used to improve rigid simulators via system identification, residual dynamics, and real-to-sim contact correction?
  • Q4 — Exploiting Imperfection: How should robot learning handle biased models? Both analytical simulators and learned world models are, fundamentally, biased approximations of reality. Rather than futilely chasing perfect physical fidelity, how can robot learning actively exploit these biases? How must control policies be designed to reason about model uncertainty, compounding errors, and exploitable simulator artifacts without failing in the real world?

Call for Papers

Submission Portal: OpenReview link to be announced

We cordially invite paper submissions relevant to the following (non-exhaustive) topics:

  • Physical Fidelity and Evaluation of World Models
  • Injecting Structural Physics Priors into Learned Representations
  • Integrating Analytical Simulation with Neural World Models
  • World Models for Planning, Control, and Decision Making
  • Learning Physical Dynamics from Video and Multimodal Data
  • State-Action Representations and Controllable Abstractions
  • System Identification, Residual Dynamics, and Real-to-Sim
  • Sim-to-Real Transfer and Long-Horizon Reasoning with World Models

Submission Guidelines

  • Deadline: To be announced.
  • Page Limit: We welcome submissions of up to 8 pages (excluding references) describing completed research, ongoing work, or benchmark datasets relevant to the workshop topics.
  • Formatting: Authors are encouraged to use the CoRL 2026 paper template. Templates from related conferences will also be accepted.
  • Reviews: Each submission will receive at least two reviews from the organizing committee and external reviewers. Authors of submitted papers are expected to help review other submissions.
  • Anonymity: All submissions will be double-anonymized.
  • Dual Submission: We welcome submissions that are under review or recently published at relevant venues.
  • Non-Archival: This workshop is non-archival. Accepted papers will be posted on the workshop website.
  • Presentation: Accepted papers will be presented in an interactive poster session, with the top 3–5 papers selected for oral spotlight presentations. Authors are expected to present in person.
  • Best Submission Award: A Best Workshop Submission Award (anticipated $500 cash prize) will be presented, subject to CoRL policy and sponsor approval.
